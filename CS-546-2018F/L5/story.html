Op-Amp’s open-loop transfer characteristic

Build a voltage divider with potentiometer and side resistors to obtain variable input voltage in the range of approximately +-11 V. Explore an open-loop Op-Amp without a negative feedback: connect divider output to the non-inverting (+) input of the Op-Amp, and connect the inverting (–) input to the ground; later reverse these connections. By slowly adjusting divider voltage near its zero value, find the voltages at which the output switches between +-11 V (the actual corner voltages are too small to be detect; you will obtain upper bounds will be much larger than those expected corner voltages).

Try to observe random oscilloscope switching between ± 14 V occurring when potentiometer output voltage is close to zero, VD ≈ 0, and try to explain the cause of these oscillations in the Report. The output oscillating between +14V and -14V when the input D has the boundary voltages in Table 1.

The random oscillations when potentiometer voltage is close to zero is most likely caused by the very small oscillating of the DC power source’s output voltages. The oscillating is so small that normally we cannot detect it, but when VD≈0, the slope between -14V and +14V is so large that it makes the very small oscillation visible.
